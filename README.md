# BRIDGE — what it is and why it exists

---

## The problem

Audi wants to work with startups. Janna and Stephanie, who run pre-series vehicle production at Audi, put it directly in the challenge brief: "the journey of an external idea into our organization is often too complex. Many steps, many approvals, little movement."

Here is what that looks like from the outside.

A founder with a working product finds a contact through a scout. They enter the process. Four months later there is still no signed contract. They don't know who is responsible for their application. Legal got involved and reset part of the process. They have no idea if Audi is serious or just exploring. Their runway is 14 months.

Meanwhile BMW, going after the same founders, gives them a supplier number in week six.

Audi has plenty of startup activity. It just has no system to turn that activity into anything.

---

## What's broken

**No front door.** A founder who finds Audi on their own has no public way to apply. The only entry is through scouts or personal contacts. The best founders, the ones with other options, go wherever the path is clearest.

**No accountability.** 27 startup pilots are running at Audi right now. But nobody is personally responsible for any individual one making it from pilot to production. Deniz Ger, who runs Startup Connect inside Audi, said it directly: "startup requests and startup connections are a soft metric for me. The better metric is going from idea to car." The current system measures the soft one.

**Pain points disappear.** A factory floor engineer sees a recurring problem. They mention it in the morning supervisor round. It travels up the department chain and vanishes. Nobody across departments knows what problems others have already flagged. Pilots duplicate, startups get pointed at the wrong people, real needs go unmatched.

---

## The BMW comparison

BMW launched Startup Garage in 2015. Startups get treated like suppliers from day one: a real supplier number, a purchase order, a capped POC timeline of under 4 months. In 2022 alone BMW completed 32 startup projects this way.

Audi launched A4nXT in 2020. In four years it has around 3 publicly documented wins. POC timelines run 3 to 6 months with no guaranteed outcome. POC Lite, their attempt at a faster track, mostly just digitised the signature step.

The 3 is not Audi being selective. It means startups are dropping out or going elsewhere before anything gets built. BMW is getting more startups to actual production, on every measure.

The reason is simple: BMW gives founders a clear signal from week one. Audi gives them months of ambiguity. Founders with options follow clarity.

BRIDGE is not a new idea. It's applying what BMW already proved works.

---

## The solution

BRIDGE is three small process changes that together create a system Audi currently doesn't have.

**The Door** is a public application form. Any founder can find it. Submit, and you get a named contact within 48 hours and a yes or no within 2 weeks. That commitment doesn't exist anywhere in Audi's process today.

**The Owner** is one named Audi employee accountable for a startup from first contact to production. Their metric is implementation, not connections made. No pilot runs without a registered owner. This is exactly the role that makes BMW's model work.

**The Map** is a shared database of pain points. Any Audi employee, including factory floor engineers, can submit a problem directly without going through their supervisor. Every pilot gets tagged to a pain point. When two departments flag the same challenge, both get notified. Department heads see all active pilots in one place.

None of these require new infrastructure. Named contact is a role. The 2-week signal is a commitment. The form is a form. The pain point field is one extra field in the existing approval flow.

## Who it's for

**Jonas Weber** is the external user. He's 31, co-founder of a 6-person startup building AI quality inspection for production lines. 800K seed raised, 14 months of runway left. He found Audi through a scout 4 months ago. Still no signed contract, no named contact, no clear next step. He can survive a no. He can't survive four months of maybe.

**Lukas Reinhardt** is the internal user. He runs Strategic Innovation and Venture Integration at Audi. He wants to show the board the startup programme is working, but his metrics are connections and requests, which he knows don't tell the real story. He has no live view of what's running across departments.

---

## What the prototype shows

Five screens. Each one makes part of the system tangible.

**Landing** — the public face of BRIDGE. States the promise. One button to apply.

**Apply** — a short three-step form. On submit you get a confirmation with your application ID and a status timeline showing what happens next.

**Founder view** — Jonas's tracker. Where his application is, who at Audi is responsible, how many days until the 2-week signal.

**Owner console** — Lukas's queue. The startups he owns, their status, days in process. At the top: implementations vs connections, so the metric shift is visible.

**Dashboard** — the shared visibility screen. 27 active pilots, how many are matched to a pain point, the pipeline from application to idea-to-car. This is the one screen that proves the whole argument: shared intelligence exists now.

When you advance a startup in the owner console, the founder view updates. When you submit a pain point, the dashboard updates. Same data, visible everywhere. That's the point.

---

## Why it's buildable

BRIDGE doesn't ask Audi to change how it builds cars. One named role per startup. One committed timeline. One extra field in an approval form. One place where pain points get written down.

On the legal side: instead of legal arriving mid-POC and resetting everything, the legal framework gets agreed before the pilot starts. Same legal process, different order.

BMW didn't build a new company to make this work. They changed their process.

---

Audi doesn't need more startups. It needs a system that doesn't lose them.
